# Starting My First Startup: Scratchbac

You know that feeling of being unable to sleep at night because your mind is whirling with thoughts?

That was what starting my first startup felt like.

It was by far one of the most exhilarating and difficult things I have ever done.
There were countless sleepless nights.
Sometimes because I was too excited about what we were building.
Sometimes because I was terrified about how we were going to keep it alive.

Nothing quite prepares you for your first startup.

Looking back, Scratchbac had many of the milestones people associate with a “successful” startup:

* I built a Telegram bot MVP that reached 20k users in a few months.
* We launched a successful [Kickstarter](https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/scratchbac/scratchbac-the-proximity-based-social-utility-app){:target="_blank"} campaign and raised $30k+.
* We were featured in the [newspaper](https://www.zaobao.com.sg/news/fukan/others/story20200726-1072028){:target="_blank"}.
* We raised $500k+ in pre-seed funding.
* We grew into a 12-person international team to build the app.
* We won awards both [locally](https://dbcsingapore.org/scratchbacfinalistsspotlight2022/){:target="_blank"} and [abroad](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1EnMaRr4xu/){:target="_blank"}.
* Eventually, we grew the app to 60k users.

This all happened over the span of 3 years.

But behind these achievements were many difficult lessons that I now look back on fondly, even though some of them were painful at the time.

Eventually, we made the decision to sunset the company.

> That was really difficult.

But I still consider Scratchbac one of the most important things I have done, because it taught me what building actually means.

Not just building a product.

Building a team.
Building trust.
Building conviction.
Building something that can survive contact with reality.

There are two lessons that stayed with me most.

# People

Before Scratchbac, I assumed the hardest parts of a startup would be external:

- Can we raise money?
- Can we get users?
- Can we build the product?
- Can we survive competition?

It turns out, the first challenge was internal.

A startup survives as long as the people inside it continue to believe that it should survive.
It can also die when that belief is no longer held together properly.

That was one of my biggest lessons.

Everything comes back to people.

We had many heated debates about where the company should go.

At first, I found these moments difficult.
When people argue passionately, it can feel like they are fighting you.
But over time, I learned to see it differently.

The heat was not always a bad thing!
It often meant that people cared.
The real skill was learning how to listen through the heat.

To understand what someone was really worried about.
To understand what they needed.
To remember that most people were not trying to “win” the argument.

They were trying to protect what they believed was best for the company.
That changed how I understood leadership.

Leadership is not about avoiding conflict.
It is about creating enough trust so that conflict can become useful.
In a startup, you are constantly debating the future.
So learning how to disagree well became one of the most important skills I developed.

Coincidentally, this is also probably what makes a good marriage.

The analogy is not that far off, because I saw my team more than I saw my partner HAHAHA.


# Vision

The second lesson was vision.

We started Scratchbac with a vision to build a community platform.
But the reality was that this was a very difficult idea to monetise.

The unit economics were not straightforward.
Many monetisation ideas risked damaging the goodwill we were trying to build inside our communities.
At the same time, we had investors, a team, and a company that needed to become financially sustainable.

That tension was hard.

On one side, we wanted to stay true to the original community-minded vision.
On the other side, we needed to find a business model that could survive.

Eventually, we found a path to revenue by serving government clients and taking on community-related projects.
That gave us sustainability.

But it also created a new question:
Was this the kind of business we wanted to build?

It was sustainable, but not obviously venture-scale.
So we were left with a very difficult choice.

Do we continue with a model that works but may not scale the way investors expect?
Or do we abandon it and keep searching for something bigger?

There was no obvious right answer.
The answer depended on what kind of company the founders wanted to build.

That was when I learned that vision is not just a nice sentence you put on a pitch deck.
Vision is a responsibility.

It is the thing you use to make hard tradeoffs when there are no clean answers.
It tells you what to protect.
It tells you what to sacrifice.
It tells you what game you are really playing.

And the bigger the team becomes, the harder this gets.
More people means more energy, more ideas, and more capability.
But it also means more responsibility to hold the bigger picture together.

Scratchbac taught me how difficult it is to be the custodian of a vision.
It also taught me how important it is.


# Closing

I know “people” and “vision” sound like broad startup lessons.

I have seen these ideas written everywhere.

I have heard other founders talk about them many times.

But they hit very differently when you are the one going through it.
Scratchbac was not a perfect success story.

We built something real.
We reached real users.
We raised real money.
We won real recognition.
We also made real mistakes.

And eventually, we had to make the hard call to sunset it.

But that is exactly why it remains one of the most impressive and formative things I have done.
It taught me how to build under pressure, how to lead people through uncertainty, and how to hold a vision even when reality kept challenging it.
